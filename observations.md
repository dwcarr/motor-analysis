While the relationship between latency and magnitude of the motion is clearly nonlinear (see Figure 2), the system itself is also inherently nonlinear given that the latency is a non-trivial function of the step size.

We see that the pitch motion is overdamped, while the yaw is somewhat underdamped. This is one of the reasons that the arrival latency of the yaw is lower than that of the pitch, the other reason being the fundamental difference in the inertial terms and possibly the effective driving strength. At lower step sizes, the yaw exhibits overshoot, while at larger steps it hunts on the way to the target (see the examples in Figures 6 and 7). This variation with motion magnitude is indication of nonlinearity, but one that is primarily related to an apparent slew rate limit of the system. We see that in the maximum instantaneous velocity chart (see system_id.html), which has a slope that appears to decrease at high angles for the yaw. In the step responses, there is an apparent delay from the time the target changes until the onset of the motion, and after the motion starts it appears consistent with the shape of linear second order system. This delay appears to be more pronounced in the pitch, which is consistent with the observed trajectory lag, suggesting that both of these could stem from a similar source.

I also observe an initial delay between the step change in the target and when the motor reaches maximum angular velocity, which is typical of driving inertial loads plus the additional lag described above. This delay does appear to exhibit some angular dependence, more than doubling over the range for the yaw, and a somwhat less pronounced effect from the pitch (see system_id.html). That is interesting, given that yaw would seem to be the more rotationally symmetric axis, but this may be partly because the higher angles also correspond to higher total motion amplitude. This variability, combined with a variable maximum velocity are the two main nonlinearities, or at least point to the two main sources of nonlinearity in the system.

The consistent trajectory lag of the position relative to the target is surprising. When the system is driven at a velocity that is much less than slew rate limited, then it would be expected that the controller would eliminate the bias between the position and the target. Instead, we see a consistent 50 ms in yaw and 70 ms in pitch, even when the target is moving at a steady and slow velocity.

The summary of the disturbances generated due to firing events is shown in Table 2. Firing from a stable position results in a disturbance that is a median 1.8 degrees in pitch and 1.7 degrees in yaw, for a total vector disturbance of 2.5 degrees. The recovery time in pitch is 260 ms and 175 ms in yaw. This is the time it takes for the disturbance to decay to less than 20% of the peak disturbance, or 0.05 degrees absolute, and remain within that range for 50 ms.

We show many examples of response to firing events while moving in the motion_disturbance.html. A few of these have a fire event recorded, but there is no apparent disturbance. When there is a disturbance, the shape is different for pitch and yaw. The pitch generally displays a double lobed response, indicating that there may be two modes of motion participating in it. This makes sense given that the pitch will likely be subjected to a delayed response from disturbances in the yaw base, which would be amplified by the moment arm of the stand (pure conjecture based on the geometry shown on the company's home page). The yaw usually only has a single lobe, but not always. Studying this further to determine if there is a systematic angular effect is recommended, but is beyond the scope of this report. We also see that fluctuations in the target can interact with the disturbance and sometimes make it larger. As such, the disturbance data from the stable target shots is more reliable than the all-shots data.
